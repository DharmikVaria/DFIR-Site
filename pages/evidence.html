Integrity Layer

Evidence Handling and Chain of Custody

Evidence handling is the foundation that makes forensic analysis trustworthy. A technically accurate finding can become weak if the investigator cannot prove where the evidence came from, how it was preserved, who handled it, and whether it remained unchanged.

What digital evidence is

Digital evidence is any data stored or transmitted by a digital system that may help establish facts in an investigation. It includes files, metadata, logs, emails, memory dumps, registry hives, browser records, cloud audit events, mobile databases, network packets, screenshots, hashes, and tool exports.

Evidence must be relevant, reliable, and explainable. Investigators must show that evidence was collected from a known source using a controlled process and that analysis was performed on verified copies wherever possible.

Write blockers

A write blocker prevents a forensic workstation from writing data to a suspect storage device. Hardware write blockers sit between the evidence drive and examiner machine, while software write-blocking methods rely on operating system controls. Hardware blockers are generally preferred for high-integrity disk acquisition because they reduce accidental modification risk.

Write blockers matter because operating systems may automatically mount drives, create hidden folders, update access timestamps, or write indexing metadata. A blocker helps maintain the original media state while the examiner creates a forensic image.

Hashing and evidence integrity

Hashing produces a fixed-length value from data. In forensics, hash values such as MD5, SHA-1, or SHA-256 are used to verify that an evidence image or exported file has not changed. Although MD5 and SHA-1 are no longer collision-resistant for cryptographic trust, they still appear in forensic workflows for legacy comparison. SHA-256 is preferred when strong integrity assurance is needed.

A common workflow is to calculate a hash before imaging, after imaging, and during later verification. Matching hashes support the claim that the forensic copy accurately represents the source at the time of acquisition.

Chain of custody

Chain of custody is the documented history of evidence possession, transfer, storage, access, and handling. It should answer: who collected the evidence, when it was collected, where it came from, how it was packaged, where it was stored, who accessed it, why access occurred, and when it was returned or transferred.

A weak chain of custody can create doubt even if the technical analysis is correct. The record should include unique evidence identifiers, device descriptions, serial numbers, photographs, seal numbers, hash values, examiner names, dates, times, and signatures where required by policy.

Admissibility and reliability

Evidence admissibility depends on jurisdiction and case context. Courts commonly consider whether evidence is relevant, authentic, reliable, and obtained legally. The examiner must be able to explain the tools and methods used, demonstrate that evidence was preserved, and show that conclusions follow from the data.

Corporate disciplinary cases may not follow the exact same courtroom rules, but they still require careful documentation because findings may later support legal action, insurance claims, or regulatory reporting.

Indian legal context

In India, electronic records may require compliance with Section 65B of the Indian Evidence Act for admissibility of electronic evidence. A Section 65B certificate is commonly used to certify the manner in which electronic records were produced and the reliability of the computer output.

The Information Technology Act, 2000 and the IT Amendment Act, 2008 provide legal recognition to electronic records and address cyber offences such as unauthorized access, identity theft, violation of privacy, and damage to computer systems. Forensic documentation should be prepared with awareness of organizational policy and applicable legal requirements.

Daubert Standard and Best Evidence Rule

The Daubert Standard, used in the United States, focuses on reliability of expert testimony. It considers whether a method can be tested, has been peer reviewed, has a known error rate, uses standards, and is generally accepted in the relevant field.

The Best Evidence Rule generally prefers original evidence when the content of a record is disputed. In digital forensics, verified forensic images and properly authenticated duplicates are often used because direct analysis of original media may risk alteration.

Evidence contamination risks

  • Booting a suspect system and changing logs, timestamps, caches, or memory state.
  • Connecting evidence media without a write blocker.
  • Running unverified tools on original evidence.
  • Mixing evidence from different cases or failing to label devices clearly.
  • Allowing unauthorized people to access evidence storage.
  • Exporting files without preserving metadata and source path references.

Common documentation mistakes

Beginners often document only final findings and forget to document process. That is risky because forensic conclusions must be reproducible. Missing tool versions, missing timezone references, unclear file paths, undocumented exports, and absent hash values make it harder to defend the work.

A good forensic note should allow another trained examiner to understand what was done, repeat the method, and compare results. Notes should separate observations from interpretation.

Practical investigation notes

Treat evidence handling as part of analysis, not as paperwork after analysis. Label devices immediately, photograph physical condition, calculate hashes early, store originals securely, and perform analysis on copies. When live response is required, document the reason because live collection changes system state by nature.
