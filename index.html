Investigation Mindset

Forensics is not just tool usage. It is controlled reasoning over preserved evidence.

A strong investigator does not simply click through tools. They ask what evidence can prove, what evidence cannot prove, how data could have changed, and whether conclusions are supported by repeatable observations.

This website is organized around the forensic lifecycle: scoping, preservation, acquisition, verification, analysis, correlation, reporting, and presentation. The goal is to help beginners understand both technical artifacts and professional investigation discipline.

Preserve first

Before analysis, protect the original evidence from accidental modification.

Verify every copy

Use hashes to prove that acquired images and exported files remain unchanged.

Document decisions

Record tools, versions, timestamps, commands, hashes, and examiner actions.

Core Workflow

A practical forensic process used across academic, corporate, and law-enforcement contexts.

Scope

Define the case question

Identify systems, users, time windows, business impact, suspected activity, and evidence priority.

Preserve

Protect evidence integrity

Isolate systems carefully, capture volatile evidence when needed, and avoid unnecessary changes.

Acquire

Create verified copies

Image disks, export logs, capture memory, collect cloud audit trails, and calculate hashes.

Analyze

Correlate artifacts

Build timelines from logs, filesystem metadata, registry keys, browser traces, memory, and packets.

Report

Explain findings

Write defensible conclusions, limitations, evidence references, and reproducible methodology.
